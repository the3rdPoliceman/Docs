Fundamentals
Force Generated between Current Carrying Wire and Magnetic Field
This is proportional to
  • Strength of magnetic field
  • Current through the wire
  • Length of wire perpendicular to magnetic field i.e. if the wire was parallel to the field no force would occur. Basic trig to work out the effective length of wire crossing the field
Force between Parallel Conductors
I.e. between wires in a coil. This is caused by the magnetic field created due to current flowing through each of the wires.
If current travels in the same direction through wires (as it would in a coil) there is an attractive force, if current is in opposite direct there's a repulsive force.
The force is proportional to the product of the currents and the length of common wire. It is inversely proportional to the distance between them.

Standards

  • IEC - the International Electrotechnical Commission standards are the uniform standards indicating the efficiency classes for electric motors
Useful conversions
  • 1HP is around 750W
Label Fields
  • HP - Horse power
  • KW - Kilowatts
  • HZ - Frequency, either 50 or 60Hz. No problem running on different frequency, it will just spin proportionally faster/slower.
  • V - Voltage expected. Can be considered rough i.e. 210 will be fine at 240 etc.
  • PH - How many phases
  • A - Current in amps.
  • CODE - Measure of the inrush (startup) current. When the motor starts, it draws a lot more current than when it is up to speed.
  • SF - How much load the motor can handle temporarily, as a ratio of the motor power
  • FR - Frame size. Can be looked up
  • AMB - Ambient temperature suitable for running the motor
  • NEMA DESIGN - Refers to torque profile e.g. if motor starts with very high torque,
  • TIME RATING/DUTY - e.g. Cont for continuous. How long you can run the motor at full load
  • ENC - Enclosure type
  • -
Stepper Motor
Types
Main features A good interactive representation of stepper motors can be found at https://en.nanotec.com/knowledge-base/stepper-motor-animation
Universal Motor
Main features
  • Similar to a DC motor, but instead of permanent magnets, electromagnets are used
  • Can run on AC or DC
  • High RPM is possible
  • High power density
  • Speed controllable through VFDs, using a thiristor or triac. Without speed control it will set its own speed based on the load
  • Have commutators and brushes
DC Motor
Types
  • DC Permanent Magnet motors - Have permanent magnets in the stator. The brushes contact the coils in turn as the motor rotates (via the commutator), gradually changing the magnetic field position in the rotor. This change in the field position puts a force on the rotor from the stator magnets
  • DC Electromagnetic motors - Operation is similar to a DC permanent magnet motor, but an electromagnet is used instead of permenent magnet to create the magnetic field
  • DC Series motor - A DC electromagnetic motor where the field windings are in SERIES with the coil windings
  • DC Shunt motor - A DC electromagnetic motor where the field windings are in PARALLEL with the coil windings
Main features
  • Stator containing either permanent or electro magnets
  • Split ring commutator
  • Multiple coils on the armature attached to the commutator
  • DC Permanent magnet can be easily used as a generator
  • Can run on batteries
Speed Control Options
  • Varible transformer to step up/down voltage, and Full bridge rectifier to convert to DC
  • PWM
  • Varying the input voltage
Brushless DC Motors
Main features
  • Multiple permanent magnets arranged in rotor
  • Rotor generally mounted outside stator windings
  • Use transistors to generate rotating magnetic field in the coils
Single Phase AC Induction Motor
Main features
  • Stator contains coils
  • No electrical connection between the rotor and the stator
  • Not easily speed controllable. Messing with input voltage is likely to increase current and cause problems with the centrifugal switch. Also, probably not a good idea to have extra current in there anyway
  • Low maintenance. Bearing are the only consumables
  • Capacitor & centrifugal switch are potential sources of problems. If motor hums, but doesn't spin, there is a problem with one of these
  • May or may not be reversible, depending on whether you have access to the starter windings
3 Phase AC Induction Motor
Main features
  • Stator contains coils
  • No electrical connection between the rotor and the stator
  • Rotating magnetic field is created by the fact the phases are 120 degrees out of phase
  • Powerful and smooth power
  • Extremely low maintenance. Bearing are the only consumables
  • Can be used with a variable-frequency drive (VFD). This allows speed control of the motor by varying the input frequency and, depending on topology, to control associated voltage or current variation
Induction Motor
Main features
  • Run on AC
  • Often require a capacitor, or even 2 for starting
Identifying a motor
Clues from the label
  • DCPM - DC Permanent Magnet
  • DC - DC Motor
  • See Hz and Phase(PH) on the label? - Probably an induction motor
  • Speed - if speed is a bit less than 3600, 1800, 1200, or 800 @60hz, or 3000, 1500, 1000, 750 @50Hz it's probably an induction motor. These speeds are the no load (and no friction) speeds of motors with 2,4,6 and 8 poles, and the motor speed on the label is typically around 5% less e.g. 1725 instead of 1800
Clues from inspection
  • See brushes on the commutator? - either a universal motor or a DC motor. So which is it? Universal motor has an electromagnet stator field
  • Is it magnetic? Most likely a permanent magnet DC motor
  • See a squirrel cage rotor inside (no electrical connection to rotor)? Probably induction. A squirrel-cage rotor consists of a cylinder of steellaminations, with aluminum or copper conductors embedded in its surface, and resembles a rodent exercise wheel.
