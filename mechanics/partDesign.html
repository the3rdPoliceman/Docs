Mounting Parts with Screws
Slots of Keyholes are good options when parts are being mounted with screws. Consideration needs to be given to how much load or shock the parts being held will be expected to withstand. Keyholes and slots are good convenience options, but if its critical your part stays in an exact place then they may be a bad idea (some big lump will come along and thump your part out of place)

Keyholes vs plain holes - Keyholes give flexibility and mean screws dont have to be removed to mount or dismount the part

Slots vs plain holes - Less positioning precision is needed when slots are used
Aligning Shafts
When shafts that run between two components are installed, getting them aligned correctly is crucial. In these cases, using some form of slots or keyholes as part of the design is a bad idea as the shafts will likely be susceptible to vibration, knocks, shocks etc.
Mounting Components on Shafts
Set Screws
These are relatively weak, and marr shafts (leaving a small raised area siilar to when you center punch something) which will prevent precision compenents being removed from the shaft.
If they are to be used, a flat must be milled or filed on the shaft, so parts can be removed once the set screw is loosened

Split collars
Split collars will create higher clamping force, a more even clamping around the bar, and will not damage it. They come in one piece and two piece varieties

One piece split collars are less fiddly, but have to be removed over the end of the shaft.

Two piece split collars give slightly higher clamping force. They can also be removed and installed without access to the end of the shaft and without moving other components, which can be an advantage
If the screws in two piece collars are in opposite directions, better balancing can be achieved

Collar clamping force increases with collar length (length along the shaft), but making the collar thicker radially does not increase its holding power. However, a larger diameter collar will enable the clamping screw head to be inset further

Using Bearings
Some details from Dan Gelbart here

Pairs
Even expensive bearings have a lot of play when used singly. When used on shafts, they always need to be used in pairs

Preloading
Bearings and other components on shafts should not be sliding around back and forwards. Components should be designed so that there is force on components keeping them in position. For eaxmple, housings should be deisnged the correct width. Preload washers can be used to take up slack where tolerences are not good Correcting Fit
For non critical applications, if bearings are slightly loose, they can be loctited into place. If they later need to be removed, heating the housing to around 150C will break the loctite bond
Strength & Stiffness
It's important to consider in each case if stregth or stiffness is the more important thing. For example, in a lathe base, you are after stiffness more than anything else. THe base will be strong enough to support tonnes of weight even though it may be only is used to machine parts up to 10kg. But if it's not very stiff, it will be useless
